Mathematics Postgraduate Seminars



The Mathematics Postgraduate Seminars are a series of fortnightly talks given by and for postgraduates in mathematics at the University of Exeter. The seminars aim to give postgraduates the opportunity to practise presenting research in an informal and friendly environment, and to learn about the research taking place across the department. All postgraduates are invited to present at and attend the seminars. If you would like to give a talk, please get in touch with me.

Seminars will take place at 2.30pm, unless indicated otherwise. The schedule for the current term is given below.


  Wednesday 2nd October 2019 (Harrison 209)
  Giulia Murtas

Magnetic reconnection is a recurring process taking place in various layers of the solar atmosphere, and plays a fundamental role in driving heating and explosive dynamics. However, unlike the processes occurring in fully-ionised coronal plasmas that have been subjects of extensive studies, still little is known about how magnetic reconnection develops in partially ionised plasmas of the lower atmosphere. In this talk I will investigate the role of partial ionisation on reconnection through the study of the coalescence instability. In a single-fluid MHD approach, this instability forms a turbulent reconnecting current sheet by driving plasmoid interaction, and is a key process that leads to fast reconnection without the addition of further anomalous resistivity terms. I will present my plasmoid simulations, focusing on the current distribution solved for a two-fluid model of the chromosphere, and then show how this new current distribution changes the dynamics of the coalescence process and the development of fast magnetic reconnection.

  Wednesday 16th October 2019 (Harrison 107)
  George Samways

Hopf-Galois structures, originally developed by Chase and Sweedler (1969), arise as a generalisation of classical Galois theory in which the underlying group is replaced by a Hopf algebra, with a corresponding fundamental theorem. Unlike the Galois group, there can be many distinct structures associated to a given extension. We are interested in counting the number of structures that exist for a given extension, in particular for a cyclic Galois extension of arbitrary degree. In this setting, the 'type' of the Hopf-Galois structures is always a group that has a C-group as an index 2 normal subgroup. In this talk I will discuss the general background and the relevant results from the literature. I will then present my work so far, in particular the number of Hopf-Galois structures of C-group type.

  Wednesday 30th October 2019 (IAIS Building LT2)
  Fabio Ferri

Let \(L/K\) be a Galois extension of number fields with Galois group \(G\). Denote by \(\mathcal{O}_L\) and \(\mathcal{O}_K\) the rings of integers of \(L\) and \(K\), respectively. A classical question is whether there exists a basis of \(\mathcal{O}_L\) over \(\mathcal{O}_K\) made of an element of \(\mathcal{O}_L\) and its conjugates via \(G\), which we call normal integral basis (NIB). This is motivated by the well-known fact that we always have a positive answer if we just take \(L\) as a \(K\)-vector space (normal basis theorem). The property of having a NIB can be restated as \(\mathcal{O}_L\) being free as an \(\mathcal{O}_K[G]\)-module, and a necessary condition for an extension to have NIB is tame ramification, a property that has to do with how primes behave when extended from \(\mathcal{O}_K\) to \(\mathcal{O}_L\). In this talk we will explain what can happen if we assume wild ramification instead, where \(\mathcal{O}_L\) cannot be free as a \(\mathcal{O}_K[G]\)-module but we can hope there is a ring bigger than \(\mathcal{O}_K[G]\) that makes this job (the "associated order"); this will be made by overviewing some classical results and giving an idea of some new ones I obtained.
