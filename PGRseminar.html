Mathematics Postgraduate Seminars



The Mathematics Postgraduate Seminars are a series of fortnightly talks given by and for postgraduates in mathematics at the University of Exeter. The seminars aim to give postgraduates the opportunity to practise presenting research in an informal and friendly environment, and to learn about the research taking place across the department. All postgraduates are invited to present at and attend the seminars. If you would like to give a talk, please get in touch with me.

Seminars will take place at 2.30pm, unless indicated otherwise. The schedule for the current term is given below.


  Wednesday 29th January 2020 (Queens LT 4.2)
  Joe MacMillan

The polynomial ring of a finite field has many properties in common with the ring of integers \(\mathbb{Z}\). Thus, many of the problems presented in the classical Analytic Number Theory setting have analogues in the function field setting. In this talk, we discuss some of the properties in common between the polynomial ring of a finite field and the ring of integers \(\mathbb{Z}\), as well as discussing some problems in Analytic Number Theory and their analogues in function fields. Finally, I will discuss some of the recent results in the function field setting.

  Wednesday 12th February 2020 (Queens LT 4.2) 3.00pm
  Douglas Coates

For the LSV map with parameter \(\frac{1}{2}< \gamma < 1\), S. Gouëzel has shown that Hölder observables \(v\) that "see" the neutral fixed point, \(v(0)\neq 0\), will satisfy a stable law. A key property that allows Gouëzel to establish this result is the fact that the return time of the LSV map to the right half of the unit interval has a regularly varying tail distribution. I will present joint work with M. Holland and D. Terhesiu in which we study "wobbly" intermittent interval maps where this regular variation is not present in the tail of the return time. In this setting we show that for a wide class of observables the stable laws of Gouëzel break down and semi-stable laws appear.

  Wednesday 26th February 2020 (Harrison 102)
  Emanuele Tovazzi

Reducing current uncertainties associated with model estimates of climate change sensitivity is actually hampered by our lack of understanding of the impact aerosol particles have on the radiative budget via their interactions with clouds. Emissions of marine organic aerosols may play a relevant role in cloud formation in regions with a high concentration of phytoplankton, for example in the Southern Ocean (SO). Comparisons between Earth System Models (ESMs) and satellite observations over the SO show that the models simulate too little reflection of shortwave radiation and this is strongly linked to modelled cloud properties. A potential cause of this bias is a missing source of aerosols in ESMs, such as marine organic emissions. In this study, we evaluate the ability of the UK Met Office ESM (UKESM1) inaccurately representing sources of marine aerosols and their subsequent transport to the baseline atmospheric pollution surface measurement station of Cape Grim. In order to compare simulations and observation of these relevant variables, a novel Lagrangian trajectory framework for evaluating ESMs has been developed. This comparison method makes use of three independent datasets: (1) the ocean surface chlorophyll concentration (a proxy for marine organic activity); (2) 10m wind speed (used as a proxy for primary emissions of sea salt aerosol); (3) simulated and measured cloud condensation nuclei concentration (CCNC) at 0.5% supersaturation at Cape Grim. A source-receptor analysis is performed to both observations and climate model data. Preliminary results from our analysis indicate that UKESM1, does not reproduce the expected temporal correlation for the wind speed data and further investigations are needed.

  Wednesday 11th March 2020 (Queens LT 4.2)
  Charlie Kirkwood

Weather forecasting continues to become ever more data intensive. Numerical Weather Prediction (NWP) models are becoming more complex, with higher resolution, and there are increasing numbers of different models in operation around the world. For the weather forecaster and the end user, the amount and complexity of information available seems to make life more complicated. Which model should we trust in a given scenario? How can we provide probabilistic answers to stakeholder questions? Which decision is the right one to make? The use of machine learning, with algorithms able to assimilate complex, task-specific information on a super-human scale, seems like a promising approach to bridge the gap between the outputs of NWP models and the true weather outcomes that we observe. Here we present an application of the Quantile Regression Forest algorithm of Athey, Tibshirani and Wager (after Meinshausen) to the post-processing of UK road temperature forecasts. By tasking the algorithm with learning a probabilistic error profile for each individual NWP model, and then combining the resultant bias-corrected probabilistic forecasts, we can convert the set of available NWP forecasts into a well calibrated probabilistic forecast for all lead times.
